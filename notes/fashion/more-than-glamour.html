More Than Glamour: A Fashion Editorial Internship at Marie Claire

During my senior year of college, while many of my classmates complained about the exhausting realities of internships, I found myself living a very different life at Marie Claire, one of Hearst's flagship fashion magazines.

Every day, I commuted between Parkview Green and the Communication University of China, where I lived. Looking back, those were some of the happiest days of my student life. I still remember the excitement of receiving a job offer from Marie Claire before I had even left the building after my interview.

A First Look Inside the Fashion Industry

As a fashion editorial intern, I soon found myself immersed in a world of photo shoots, designer collections, celebrity interviews, and ever-changing trends. Fashion editorial work depends more on creativity and inspiration than on rigid schedules. At Hearst's fashion magazine division, it was not unusual to see colleagues enjoying brunch well into the afternoon. From interns to senior editors, people enjoyed a level of flexibility that reflected the culture of Marie Claire—free-spirited, creative, and confident.

One of the most fascinating places in the office was the lounge. Celebrities frequently stopped by between shoots, and it was not uncommon to find yourself standing next to people you had previously seen only on magazine covers or television screens. At first, these encounters felt surreal. As time went on, I became a familiar face to several talent managers. Our casual conversations often led to snippets of insider gossip and behind-the-scenes stories that never reached the public.

The Work of a Fashion Editor

Fashion editors were expected to understand international brands inside and out and to anticipate trends months before they reached consumers. They also needed a deep familiarity with fashion history, film, and popular culture. Shows such as Sex and the City, Gossip Girl, and The Devil Wears Prada were more than entertainment—they were part of the cultural vocabulary of the industry.

Keeping up with fashion meant understanding not only what was trending, but also the ideas and cultural influences behind it. In 2018, for example, Dior continued to champion feminist themes under Maria Grazia Chiuri, Valentino remained known for its romantic tulle and couture-inspired elegance, Gucci's maximalist aesthetic dominated fashion conversations, and Balenciaga's oversized Triple S sneakers became one of the defining trends of the year. As a fashion editor, these were the kinds of topics you were expected to discuss effortlessly—and endlessly.

Beyond trend forecasting and fashion knowledge, magazine work also meant accompanying editors on interviews and photo shoots. Whenever the assignment involved a celebrity I admired, I would eagerly prepare myself for the opportunity to meet someone I had long followed from afar. One of my most memorable assignments was representing the magazine at actor Yang Yang's birthday celebration. Sitting in the VIP section of Beijing's Workers' Gymnasium, I watched the event from only a short distance away.

Behind the Scenes

Yet behind the glamour, fashion editorial work was often demanding. Long nights organizing garments before and after shoots were routine, and handling luxury fashion pieces required constant caution. Even a Givenchy camisole could cost more than $2,000, making every garment feel like something that needed to be handled with extreme care.

Photo shoots often lasted until midnight. As interns, we followed photographers from location to location, hauling suitcases packed with garments and helping prepare celebrities for the camera. Yet these demanding moments also offered unique opportunities. Helping an idol straighten a bow tie or writing lines for a promotional video brought a sense of excitement and accomplishment that was difficult to describe.

Beyond the Runway

Outside the office, Beijing became part of the experience. Walking home through the city's business district, past landmarks such as Parkview Green and the iconic CCTV Headquarters, I gradually felt myself becoming part of a world I had once only imagined.

Another Hearst publication, ELLE, occupied the office next door. Over dinners and late nights at the office, its interns would often share stories from overseas assignments and fashion events they attended with their editors. Some of those stories seemed straight out of a movie: arriving at the Prince's Palace of Monaco by helicopter, dining atop the Grand Palais beneath a roof that suddenly opened to reveal a sky full of stars, or spending the night in a presidential suite once occupied by Grace Kelly. Listening to these stories, I was constantly reminded of how extraordinary—and occasionally surreal—the fashion industry could be.

More Than Glamour

Life at Marie Claire came with its share of perks, including invitations to celebrity events and access to the glamorous side of the industry. Yet what stayed with me most was not the glamour itself.

During my time at Hearst, I became a sponge, absorbing everything I could from the talented editors around me. I learned how fashion magazines operate, how stories are created, and how ideas are transformed into images and experiences. Some days I was helping prepare garments worth thousands of dollars for a shoot; on others, I was writing lines for celebrity videos or assisting behind the scenes as cover stories came to life. Each assignment offered a glimpse into a world that had once felt impossibly distant.

One day, I encountered Angelica Cheung, the editor-in-chief of Vogue China, in the elevator. Nervously, I said hello. To my surprise, she responded warmly. Back in the office, my supervisor laughed and told me that many editors would have been too intimidated to approach her. Among them, Angelica was regarded as a formidable figure—an icon whose influence extended across the Chinese fashion industry. The fact that she had greeted a young intern so graciously made the moment all the more memorable.

Moments like these defined my time at Hearst.

Looking back, what stays with me most is not the celebrities, the luxury brands, or the exclusive events, but the feeling of growth—the sense that a larger world was gradually opening itself to me.

For that, I remain grateful to Hearst.
And to Marie Claire.
